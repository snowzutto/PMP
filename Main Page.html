PMP Exam Overview and Study Practices

The Project Management Professional (PMP) exam is a certification exam that tests your knowledge and skills in project management. The exam is designed to ensure that you have the necessary skills to successfully manage projects from start to finish, including planning, executing, monitoring and controlling, and closing. The exam covers the following three domains:

  • People (42% of exam questions)
  • Process (50% of exam questions)
  • Business Environment (8% of exam questions)

To prepare for the PMP exam, it is recommended that you study the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK) guide, which is published by the Project Management Institute (PMI). The PMBOK guide provides a framework for project management and covers the knowledge areas and processes that are tested on the exam. In addition to studying the PMBOK guide, it is also recommended that you take practice exams and participate in study groups or courses.

Question Types

The questions on the PMP exam can be categorized into three types:

  • Situational: These questions present a scenario or problem, and you must use your project management knowledge to choose the best course of action.
  • Formula-based: These questions require you to use mathematical formulas to solve a problem.
  • Knowledge-based: These questions test your general understanding of project management concepts.

In addition to the multiple-choice questions, the new PMP exam also includes other question types such as multiple response, matching, hotspot, and fill-in-the-blank questions.

Recommended Study Practices

PMI recommends that candidates preparing for the PMP exam follow a structured study plan that includes a combination of self-study, instructor-led training, and practice exams. PMI offers a variety of resources to help candidates prepare, including the PMP Exam Prep eLearning Course, the PMP Exam Prep Book, and the PMP Exam Simulator.

It is also recommended that candidates create a study plan that includes regular study sessions, practice exams, and review of key project management concepts and terminology. Candidates should also seek out opportunities to apply their knowledge in real-world project management scenarios through volunteer work, internships, or work projects.

  • Read and study the PMBOK guide thoroughly.
  • Take practice exams to become familiar with the types of questions that will be on the exam.
  • Participate in a study group or course to discuss and review exam material with others.
  • Review your exam results and identify areas where you need to focus your studying.
  • Manage your time effectively during the exam to ensure that you have enough time to answer all questions.

Project Management Institute (PMI)

The Project Management Institute (PMI) is a global professional association for project managers. Founded in 1969, the PMI is dedicated to advancing the profession of project management and promoting its value in a variety of industries and contexts. The PMI provides a range of resources and services to its members, including professional development opportunities, networking events, and certification programs.

One of the most well-known certifications offered by the PMI is the Project Management Professional (PMP) certification. The PMP certification is widely recognized as a mark of professional achievement and is highly valued by employers in a variety of industries. To become certified, individuals must meet education and professional experience requirements and pass the PMP exam.

The PMP exam is a four-hour, computer-based exam that tests the knowledge and skills required to manage projects effectively. The exam is based on the PMP Exam Content Outline, which is organized around three main domains: People, Process, and Business Environment. The exam consists of 180 questions that cover a range of project management concepts, tools, and techniques. The questions are a combination of multiple-choice, multiple-response, matching, hotspot, and fill-in-the-blank questions.

The PMP exam underwent some significant changes in January 2021. The changes were made to ensure that the exam continues to reflect the current state of the project management profession and the evolving needs of project managers. Some of the key changes that were made to the PMP exam include: New Exam Content Outline: The PMP exam content outline was updated to reflect the changing needs of the project management profession. The new outline is organized around three main domains: People, Process, and Business Environment. New Question Types: The new PMP exam includes a variety of question types, including multiple choice, multiple response, matching, hotspot, and fill-in-the-blank questions. Emphasis on Agile: The new exam places greater emphasis on Agile and other iterative approaches to project management, reflecting the growing importance of these methods in the project management profession. Role of the Project Manager: The new exam places greater emphasis on the role of the project manager as a leader and communicator, reflecting the evolving expectations of the profession. Continuing Education Requirements: In addition to passing the exam, PMPs are now required to complete 60 professional development units (PDUs) every three years to maintain their certification.
